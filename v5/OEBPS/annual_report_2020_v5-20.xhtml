Temple University Libraries

Beyond the Page, the Libraries’ free public programming series, fosters conversations of social, scholarly, and educational value as we invite artists, writers, scholars, and experts in a variety of fields to address topics of importance to Temple and the surrounding community.

This past academic year, the opening of the new Charles Library inspired us to explore the history and future of the book. We examined how books have evolved, how libraries adapt to meet user needs, and what Charles Library makes possible for our staff and patrons.

Public Programs

In addition to this curated series, we offered a variety of panels, collaborations, workshops, performances, and lectures, such as our award-winning Beyond the Notes concerts and long-running Chat in the Stacks programs.

And while we had to postpone some of our spring programs amid the closing of the library buildings, we were able to swiftly move a number of events and workshops online in order to continue to offer academic and cultural programs to our users.

Faculty and graduate students delve into the history and future of the book

We invited several faculty members, graduate students, and alumni to Charles Library to discuss their own research on that most iconic of library resources: the book. We held two events—one in the fall and one in the spring—and heard about work being done at the Tyler School of Art and Architecture, the Lewis Katz School of Medicine, and in the English department via a series of fascinating lightning talks. SCRC staff also prepared pop-up exhibits featuring archival materials as part of these talks.

Building the 21st century library: reflections on a project completed

In 2016, we launched a multi-year program series in collaboration with the Tyler School of Art and Architecture to explore the process of building a 21st century library as we embarked on our own project here at Temple. And this past spring, we held the culminating event in this series in our new Charles Library event space with the architects, project managers, and other professionals involved in the design and construction of the new building reflecting on their work and the finished project.

Hands-on workshops exploring data art

As part of Love Data Week in February, the Ginsburg Health Sciences Library invited artists Mina Zarfsaz and Angela McQuillan to lead a hands-on workshop exploring the idea that we need to interpret data for it to have a cultural value. Participants learned how data can be represented using sound and created their own art. Later that week Mina Zarfsaz also gave a talk and led a demo with her students at Charles Library on Main Campus.
