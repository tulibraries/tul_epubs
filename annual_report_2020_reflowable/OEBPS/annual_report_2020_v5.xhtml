About the Libraries

Temple University Libraries serves the Temple community and beyond, including more than 39,000 students; over 2,000 full-time faculty; and researchers and visitors on Main, Center City, and Health Sciences Center campuses in Philadelphia and on our Ambler and Harrisburg campuses. We are committed to providing research and learning services, offering open access to our facilities and information resources, and fostering innovation and experimentation. Our collections total more than four million physical and digital titles, over 275,000 print and electronic journal subscriptions, and more than 700 research databases. We also collect, preserve, and provide access to a broad universe of special collections, including rare books, manuscripts, archives, photographs, and more. As part of our library enterprise, the award-winning Temple University Press supports our mission to advance learning and scholarship.

Dean Joe Lucia

Note: The 2020 fiscal year ran from July 1, 2019 to June 30, 2020. Many of the activities and images depicted in this report occurred prior to the COVID-19 pandemic.

Dear friends,

I hope this message finds you well. It has been a challenging year, and certainly not the one I envisioned as I, along with so many others, looked forward to our first year in a brand new library building.

As you may know, we opened the new Charles Library on Temple’s Main Campus last August. There was much excitement around this dynamic, inspiring new space, as well as a share of media interest and acclaim. But more importantly, after its grand debut, Charles Library became a beacon, drawing in students, faculty, staff, and visitors from around the city, region, and country through its doors and up through its four, light-filled floors.

But, like for all of us, the COVID-19 pandemic changed things. From March until August, our library buildings were closed and we shifted much of our work online. It was—to be honest—disheartening to see such a magnificent building as Charles standing empty.

Yet, we all know a library is about so much more than its physical presence. In the midst of upheaval, our library staff moved quickly to provide much-needed, remote services and resources. You will read more about that work in this report.

This report also highlights initiatives, projects, and accomplishments of the past fiscal year, from new special collections acquisitions and titles published by Temple University Press to major news items, staff achievements, and library statistics.

I’m proud of the work we’ve done during this time, and I also look toward the future with hope. If this experience has taught us anything, it’s that resilience is one of our greatest strengths.

Thank you for taking the time to read about our year at the Libraries. Your continuing interest in and support of our work is truly appreciated, and I wish you health and safety as we navigate this new territory together.

With warm regards,

Joe Lucia

Charles, Interrupted

In August 2019, we celebrated the opening of the new Charles Library on Temple’s Main Campus. This new building boldly envisions the future of academic libraries, and is a powerful testament to Temple’s commitment to teaching and learning.

As we prepared our 2019 Annual Report, we were already looking forward to the 2020 issue, when we could share stories from our first year in Charles Library with you. But that first year was interrupted in March 2020, when Temple moved classes fully online in an effort to keep the Temple community safe and to slow the spread of COVID-19. Charles Library, along with all our other library locations, was closed to the public until August of this year.

Despite these very challenging circumstances, we remained committed to serving our patrons and providing important resources and services even as our physical locations were closed. Library staff worked quickly to offer expanded access to online journals and databases, moved workshops and reference consultations online, contributed to a universitywide taskforce to produce PPE for Temple University Hospital, created tutorials and other online learning resources, and more.

As you read this report, Charles Library is open once more. We have enacted a variety of measures to ensure the safety of our visitors and staff at Charles and all our libraries, and we will continue to adapt and persevere in the coming months. In the meantime, we are grateful for this opportunity to share with you a few of our favorite memories from this first year in Charles and to celebrate some of the spaces, features, and people that make it such a special place.

A grand opening

We hosted a formal dedication ceremony for Charles Library on September 12, 2019. Representatives from the city and state were in attendance, along with university leaders, trustees, donors, architects, representatives from the student body, and the library’s namesake Steve Charles, KLN ‘80.

The following week, we held a massive all-campus celebration and welcomed students, faculty, staff, and members of the community into the new space. The day was filled with musical performances, 3D printing and VR demonstrations in the Loretta C. Duckworth Scholars Studio, a poetry reading by alumna Yolanda Wisher, and informal presentations about our services and other library locations.

Notable awards

In its first year, Charles Library received numerous accolades, including the American Library Association (ALA)/International Interior Design Association (IIDA) 2020 Library Interior Design Award—Best in Competition, the American Institute of Architects (AIA) Education Facility Design Award, and Interior Design magazine’s Best of the Year Award. Charles Library was also featured in American Libraries magazine’s design showcase issue, published in September 2020, and profiled in the Wall Street Journal, Architectural Digest, and the Philadelphia Inquirer.

Meet me at the Charles

Situated in the heart of Main Campus, Charles Library (affectionately referred to as “the Charles”) is an inviting space where students, faculty, staff, and members of the community want to gather. Prior to the campus closures related to COVID-19, groups on and off campus reserved spaces for meetings, while the university used Charles Library as a site for many universitywide events, including Experience Temple Day and the faculty and staff holiday gathering.

Charles Library’s main service point is the One Stop Assistance desk. Located on the first floor, this bustling spot is now the main point of contact for library users on Main Campus. There, we help users find and check out materials, connect with a subject librarian, or point people in the right direction.

During the fall and first part of the spring semester, you’d most often find the building busy with activity, from ongoing art installations to pop up concerts in the atrium to guided tours throughout the building. Not to mention all the open study areas and reservable study rooms where students and faculty were working independently or in groups.

The building’s outdoor spaces proved to be popular spots for collaborations, from our work with the Free Library of Philadelphia to sign visitors up for library cards outside the 13th Street entrance to a Mercury in transit viewing hosted by the College of Science and Technology on the fourth floor terrace.

Singular design

The first thing many of us notice about Charles Library is its stunning architecture. Designed by world-renowned architectural firm Snøhetta in collaboration with Stantec, a local leader in design and construction, stand-out features include the building’s unique geometry, its clever integration of spaces, and the amazing amount of daylight it brings inside.

Inside the building, visitors are struck by the intriguing combination of wood and concrete. A unique domed atrium opens up views throughout the building and acts as a wayfinding device. An oculus draws you up to the light-filled fourth floor, where open stacks are flanked by study rooms and quiet reading areas, while a glazed window at the third floor, cut into the warm wooden dome, provides a point of introspection, looking out over the sun-drenched lobby and to the campus beyond.

Innovative technology

One of Charles Library’s most unique features is its Automated Storage and Retrieval System, which we call the BookBot. The BookBot houses the majority of our general collection and a portion of SCRC archives in high density storage bins. This allows more room for people, flexible spaces, services, and other resources throughout the building.

To retrieve books, patrons can search on our website and “order” books that are located in the BookBot. A robotic arm retrieves and delivers their orders to a library staff member. We still have a browsing collection of approximately 180,000 books located on the fourth floor.

At Charles Library, flexible space means mobile technology. Temple students, faculty, and staff can check out laptops and portable battery chargers from kiosks located on each floor and then return them to any kiosk in the library or across campus.

Another innovative space is on the third floor of Charles: the Loretta C. Duckworth Scholars Studio. Formerly the Digital Scholarship Center in Paley Library, the Duckworth Scholars Studio is a hub for cutting-edge creativity and collaboration, housing a variety of VR equipment, 3D printers, a Makerspace, high-end computing, and more. Duckworth Scholars Studio staff also host a variety of workshops and events, including a tabletop gaming series.

Under one (green) roof

For the first time ever, the Temple University Press office is now located in our Main Campus library. Known for publishing award-winning titles in the social sciences and the humanities, as well as regional titles, the Press is also involved in a variety of open access initiatives. Its new central location at Charles offers more opportunities for connection and collaboration and signals the Libraries’ and Press’ shared commitment to advancing scholarship.

Charles Library is also the new home of the Student Success Center (SSC), the university’s comprehensive, “one-stop-shop” academic support center serving graduate and undergraduate students. The center offers a wide range of services, including academic coaching, STEM and writing tutoring, as well as help for students who are developing a knowledge of English or other languages. The new SSC location makes it easier for students to access multiple services in one spot.

Another important space in Charles Library is the Special Collections Research Center (SCRC). The SCRC is the principal repository for and steward of the Libraries’ rare books, manuscripts, archives, and University records, and its upgraded facilities in Charles Library include the Albert M. Greenfield Special Collections Research Center Reading Room, a dedicated instruction room, a conservation lab, a rare book vault, and compact shelving.

Guided by sustainability

Sustainability is truly at the heart of Charles Library. Perhaps most noticeable is the massive green roof, which covers approximately 70% of the building’s roof surface and is one of the largest in Pennsylvania. Visible from many vantage points on the fourth floor, this meadow-like landscape is filled with ornamental grasses, perennials, groundcovers, bulbs, and sedums that are all native or naturalized to the region and provide a rich urban habitat for pollinators. The green roof also collects and stores most precipitation, which along with underground basins, mitigates the impact on the City’s stormwater system.

Charles was also designed to use less heating and cooling energy, and large glass windows reduce the need for artificial lighting during daylight hours. All the wood inside the building has been certified by the Forest Stewardship Council, while all the toilets and sinks in Charles meet low water usage requirements.

A story behind every name

We’ve received numerous gifts to the Library Endowment to support Charles Library now and well into the future. Some of these gifts from our generous donors were made to name important spaces for study, research, and student engagement in the new building.

Here are the stories behind just a few of the named spaces that truly speak to the heart and mission of Charles Library:

  • Charles Library was named for entrepreneur and university trustee Steve Charles, KLN ’80, in recognition of his historic gift to the Libraries.
  • The Loretta C. Duckworth Scholars Studio was named in memory of Temple University trustee and Library Board of Visitors member Loretta C. Duckworth, CLA ‘62, ‘65, TYL ‘92, by her husband, the late W. Joseph Duckworth.
  • The Albert M. Greenfield Foundation made a gift to name the Albert M. Greenfield Special Collections Research Center Reading Room. Mr. Greenfield was a philanthropist, political activist, and board member of many civic and philanthropic organizations, and our SCRC holds records documenting his many contributions to Philadelphia. The Greenfield Foundation also donated a portrait of Albert Greenfield that is on display in the reading room.
  • The Frederic S. Schroeder and Family Green Roof was named by Library Board of Visitors Chair Sandra Lea Cadwalader, Law ‘74. Ms. Cadwalader also named the Lea Aspinwall Cadwalader Dean’s Office in honor of her mother.
  • Russell Ring, whose family has deep ties to Temple and its history, named the Carlyle Conwell Ring, Jr. Staff Meeting Room in honor of his father. This space is part of the SCRC, which houses rich archives associated with Mr. Ring’s family and the university’s history.
  • Harriette Hirsch has long supported the Libraries’ children’s literature collection and in Charles Library, the Dr. Paul and Mrs. Harriette Newman Hirsch, EDU ’55, ’61 Instruction Room is next to our collection of children’s literature on the fourth floor.
  • The Vogel-Denebeim Family Special Collections Research Center Conservation Lab was named by Temple alumnus and Library Board of Visitors member Mark Vogel, CST ‘76, in honor of his family.

The Libraries Respond to COVID-19

During the spring and summer 2020 sessions, we provided critical services and resources amid our university’s quick transition to online teaching and learning in response to the COVID-19 pandemic.

While some of these responses were specific to the building closures, we are carrying others into the fall semester as Temple offers mostly online courses. We supported (and continue to support) students and faculty by:

  • Working to provide expanded access to ebook and journal collections that had not previously been accessible to our users.
  • Applying for and receiving HathiTrust’s Emergency Temporary Access Service (ETAS), which offered expanded access to digitized versions of items in our print collections. As part of this service, Temple students, faculty, and staff had online reading access to about 40% of our print collection while our buildings were closed.
  • Working directly with patrons to help them find digital copies of books and articles, where available.
  • Moving information literacy instruction and research consultations with subject librarians online.
  • Creating new research guides, tutorials, and other online learning resources, and collaborating with Temple’s Center for the Advancement of Teaching to create a library support guide to aid in the transition to online teaching.
  • Offering online workshops on a variety of topics, from mapping COVID-19 in Philadelphia and the region to managing archival research images to best practices in poster presentations.
  • Creating a new webpage for users to explore our fully online library resources, including collections and support.
  • Launching a creative writing contest around the theme of “Interruption,” to foster community while our locations were closed. The contest was open to Temple affiliates and the surrounding community and select works appear in our Short Édition short story dispenser in Charles Library.
  • Extending due dates and eliminating late fines for library materials during building closures and into the fall semester, and expanding alumni access to digital materials.

In the Makerspace: university initiative to make PPE

The Libraries’ Makerspace Manager David Ross was part of a group of Temple faculty, staff, and students who launched a universitywide initiative in March to produce personal protective equipment (PPE) for Temple University Hospital. Named the Temple University COVID-19 Assistance Team (TUCAT), this cross-disciplinary effort spanned from the College of Engineering and College of Science and Technology to Tyler School of Art and Architecture to Temple Libraries, Temple Health, and the Office of the Vice President for Research.

At the Loretta C. Duckworth Scholars Studio in Charles Library, Ross used 3D printers, laser cutters, and other equipment in our Makerspace to create and produce molds and face shields as part of this important project. Ross and his collaborators took a unique approach to PPE creation: rather than 3D printing the masks themselves, they printed molds out of which face shields could be cast. This process saved time and created equipment that could be reused. For a time, Ross was the only library staff member working out of Charles Library.

Major News Round Up

While opening the new Charles Library and responding to the evolving COVID-19 pandemic, we were involved in a number of other important activities and initiatives. We have highlighted some of these major news items on the following pages.

In the same month that we opened Charles Library, we also launched a brand new library website after several years of development and user testing. It serves to enhance discovery of library resources, and to promote the use of services and amenities both online and within our walls.

As we celebrated the 100th anniversary of women’s right to vote this year, staff from the SCRC and Metadata and Digitization Services continued to work on “In Her Own Right: A Century of Women’s Activism, 1820-1920,” a project of the Philadelphia Area Consortium of Special Collections Libraries (PACSCL). “In Her Own Right” is a multi-phase, multi-grant-funded project to digitize and make accessible primary sources documenting women working for women’s rights in the century leading up to women getting the vote in Philadelphia and beyond.

Library staff worked with the College of Public Health to help publish the first issue of their open access online journal, CommonHealth. A peer-reviewed journal, CommonHealth publishes papers of interest to public health scholars. The Libraries are hosting the journal via an open source platform, and all articles are immediately freely available to read, download, and share. This collaborative project showcases the groundbreaking work being conducted by our Temple students and faculty.

The Libraries launched TUScholarShare, the university’s digital archive for compiling, storing, and sharing the wide range of scholarly works created in the course of research and teaching at Temple. The repository makes Temple scholarship freely available online to a global audience, with the goal of advancing knowledge and learning.

In March, we installed a new exhibit in Charles Library: a preview of the forthcoming graphic novel Black Lives Always Mattered!: Hidden African American Philadelphians in the Twentieth Century. The graphic novel, which has been supported by The Pew Center for Arts & Heritage, is a project of the Charles L. Blockson Afro-American Collection. The exhibit provides a glimpse of the beautiful artwork featured in the graphic novel scheduled for release in 2021 and is on display in Charles Library through November 2020, after which it will be on display at the Blockson Collection.

We celebrated the winners of the 16th annual Livingstone Undergraduate Research Awards. While we were unable to hold this year’s ceremony due to the pandemic, we created a special website (sites.temple.edu/livingstone) to showcase the incredible work of these talented undergraduates and recreated the experience of the live event by linking to the winner’s work, getting faculty statements about the winners, and sharing the winners’ reflections on their projects.

The Temple University Beasley School of Law Library hosted its first ever open house in September 2019. The event allowed students and faculty to mingle with librarians, vendors, and each other.

Librarians in the Learning and Research Services department launched a podcast and video series exploring behind the scenes work at the Libraries, available on our Human Sciences blog at sites.temple.edu/humansciences.

The Libraries continued to champion and support open access through the Open Access Publishing Fund, North Broad Press, and the Textbook Affordability Project. The first North Broad Press open textbook, Structural Analysis by Felix Udoeyo, was published this year.

At the end of the spring semester, we teamed up with the Philly Goat Project to livestream goats from their farm on the Libraries’ Instagram account. This goat project, which drew more than 80 viewers, was a creative, safe alternative to the therapy dogs we typically bring in to help students destress during exams.

The Ginsburg Health Sciences Library unveiled its newest resource for students, faculty, and researchers: the Presentation Practice Studio. The new studio is tailor-made for the Temple community at the Health Sciences Center to practice public speaking, prepare for academic or professional presentations, and experiment with prerecorded video to augment presentations.

In late May, the SCRC issued an open call for quarantine mail art as a way to document experiencing the COVID-19 pandemic.

The Charles L. Blockson Afro-American Collection launched the Dr. Ione D. Vargus Lecture Series, a programming series established by Dr. Ione Vargus. In January, the inaugural program featured Robert W. Bogle, president and CEO of The Philadelphia Tribune, and radio personality W. Cody Anderson in conversation.

Ambler Campus Library staff prepared materials to help Temple’s Landscape Architecture and Horticulture students and faculty to imagine, design, and build their 2020 Philadelphia Flower Show exhibit. Temple’s exhibit, “Course of Action: A Radical Tack for Suburban Tracts,” won top honors, including a PHS Gold Medal, the Alfred M. Campbell Memorial Trophy, and a PHS Gold Medal Plant Award.

In November, we hosted our first conference in Charles Library: OpenCon Philly, a free one-day series of panels and interactive workshops for idea exchange and learning around open access, open education, and open data. Members of the Libraries’ Scholarly Communications Strategic Steering Team worked in collaboration with librarians from across the region to put on this event.

As part of Love Data Week 2020, the Ginsburg Health Sciences Library unveiled its first art installation. The Tempestries exhibit was a series of crocheted tapestries from the Schuylkill Center for Environmental Education that used climate data and colored yarn to depict changes in average temperatures in Philadelphia from the 19th to the 21st centuries.

As part of a cross-departmental project involving Metadata and Digitization Services, the Loretta C. Duckworth Scholars Studio, the Special Collections Research Center, and Library Technology Development, we worked to make digitized books from the Paskow Science Fiction Collection available via HathiTrust.

Temple University Press books and authors won 13 awards, including a “Best Book of 2019” award by Foreign Affairs for Challenging Beijing’s Mandate of Heaven by Ming-sho Hos and a CHOICE Outstanding Academic Title award for Architectures of Revolt edited by Mark Shiel.

In December 2019, the Pennsylvania Historical and Museum Commission (PHMC) awarded the Charles L. Blockson Afro-American Collection a $10,000 Historical and Archival Records Care (HARC) grant. The grant will support conservation and preservation of the negatives in the John W. Mosley Photograph Collection, which provides a window into African American cultural, political, religious, and economic history in Philadelphia.

The Temple University Beasley School of Law Library received a Presidential Plaque of Appreciation in July 2020 from the American Association of Law Libraries.

New Acquisitions

The Libraries’ special collections include the Special Collections Research Center (SCRC) and the Charles L. Blockson Afro- American Collection. The SCRC houses the Libraries’ rare books, manuscripts, archives, university records, and important regional collections such as the Urban Archives and the Philadelphia Jewish Archives. The Blockson Collection holds a variety of rare and contemporary publications, photographs, archives, and manuscripts documenting African and African American life and history. Recent acquisitions help build these valuable collections for students, scholars, and members of the broader community engaged in research.

Select Special Collections Research Center (SCRC) Acquisitions

The SCRC added over 335 feet of material to its holdings during the year, including the following new collections:

Charles Austermuhl donated his collection of Walter de la Mare (1873-1956) first editions, association copies, dedication copies, and letters, gathered over 30 years of dedicated collecting. Poet and author de la Mare’s work has long been a collecting interest in Temple’s special collections, starting with first editions before World War I and a significant purchase of letters by Library Director Walter Hausdorfer in 1959.

Jonathan Jager, TYL ’68, served with the 7th PsyOp (Psychological Operations) Battalion in Da Nang, Vietnam in 1969-70. His collection of over 100 of original drawings and printed pieces document that work and includes captions he wrote for an exhibition with additional context about the images.

Among the small theatre company records we have received recently are those of Flashpoint Theatre Company (2004-2018), a Philadelphia company that focused on performing contemporary works with a social focus. They are perhaps best known for their annual productions of David Sedaris’s SantaLand Diaries. The records include administrative files, posters, ephemera, clippings and reviews, and production photographs.

At the end of spring semester and during summer session, Professor Mabel Rosenheck’s Intellectual Heritage classes created digital COVID scrapbooks reflecting their experiences during the pandemic.

An SCRC priority is to continue to gather archives created by Philadelphia’s many neighborhood associations. A recent addition is the records of Cedar Park Neighbors (1963-2013), including board minutes and bylaws; activity binders; grant, project, and subject files; newsletters; and website. CPN is just west of University City.

Select Charles L. Blockson Afro-American Collection Acquisitions

The Blockson Collection recently acquired the resolution by the Philadelphia City Council on the murder of Emmett Till and the coverage in Life Magazine. This document, signed by City Council President James H.J. Tate with ink stamp of Councilman Raymond Pace Alexander, states that the murder of Emmett Till caused “great shame and loss” and his trial was a “tragic miscarriage of justice.” The resolution congratulates Life Magazine for its October 10, 1955 issue, “In Memoriam Emmett Till.” Till was a 14-year-old Chicagoan visiting relatives in Mississippi, who was tortured, shot, and killed in August 1955, for allegedly whistling at a white woman. At the trial, his murderers were acquitted.

Reverend Nelson Carter (1808-1856) was an African Methodist Episcopal Church minister in Western Pennsylvania. This collection includes the family Bible, printed in 1847 by the American Bible Society, with a five-page handwritten insert of family births and deaths from 1808 through 1950. The family papers consist of photographs, handwritten and typed letters, a memorandum book, a ten-page family history with a two-page narrative of Jane Carter (1808-1900) under slavery, and two typed letters by her lawyer son William A. Butler of Washington, PA, one of which was to the Gazette Times stating that neither he nor his family had ever been “insulted because of color” in the town.

Our faculty and students, who often research historical representations of race in America, find cultural products such as advertisements excellent teaching tools. A new addition to the collection is: You Got to Have Soul! An advertisement for sheen conditioner, shampoo, afro combs, and other products by LaCrosby Beauty Supply Co., Inc. Jose LaCrosby (1927-2016) was a nationally renowned African American hairdresser in San Francisco who owned the company. The advertisement was published around 1970 at the height of the Black Power and Black Cultural Movements, when activists and intellectuals promoted the wearing of natural African hairstyles and the use of African influenced fashion.

Black Control of the Black Community: Vote Socialist Workers in 1970 is a document in keeping with the 1966 ten-point Black Panther Party platform, which demanded equal justice in all areas of life for African Americans and Black control of the Black community.

Another new acquisition is the Elaine Brown, Democrat City Council District #3 poster, which reads “Elect Elaine: Vote April 15th.” This poster was for Elaine Brown’s unsuccessful campaign to be on the Oakland City Council in 1973. Born in Philadelphia, Brown grew up on York Street in North Philadelphia. She graduated in 1961 from Philadelphia High School for Girls. After briefly attending Temple University, she moved to Los Angeles in 1965 and joined the Black Panther Party. A singer, Brown recorded two albums containing Black Panther themed songs and became a member of the Black Panther Party’s Central Committee. From 1973 through 1977, Elaine Brown was the chair of the Black Panther Party.

New Temple University Press Publications

Temple University Press is a leading publisher of books in the social sciences and the humanities, as well as books about Philadelphia and the Delaware Valley region. These selected titles represent a range of new Press offerings from the past fiscal year.

Shakespeare and Trump

Jeffrey R. Wilson

In this new book, Jeffrey Wilson asks the question: should we draw an analogy between Shakespeare’s tyrants—Richard III, Julius Caesar, Macbeth, and King Lear—and Donald Trump? From reading Trump’s first 100 days in office against Netflix’s House of Cards to exploring a summer 2017 Shakespeare in the Park production of Julius Caesar wherein an assassination of a Trump-ian leader caused corporations to withdraw sponsorship, Wilson uncovers a surprising—and bizarre—relationship between the provincial English playwright and the billionaire President of the United States, ostensibly a medieval king living in a modern world. The comparison reveals a politics that blends villainy and comedy en route to tragedy.

“Wilson’s take on Richard Gloucester, Frank Underwood, and Trump is as deft as it is insightful...it shows in compelling detail how Richard and Underwood win complicity from their on- and off-stage audiences, and then demonstrates how Trump and his various courtier-goons have used many of the same techniques.... Shakespeare and Trump is unmistakably personal; it is engaging, energetic, open and easy to read.”

—The Times Literary Supplement

Invisible People: Stories of Lives at the Margins

Alex Tizon, Edited by Sam Howe Verhovek, Foreword by Jose Antonio Vargas

Every human being has an epic story, according to the late Pulitzer Prize–winning writer Alex Tizon, who spent his career telling the epic stories of marginalized people. Edited by Tizon’s friend and former colleague Sam Howe Verhovek, Invisible People collects the best of Tizon’s rich, empathetic accounts—including the Atlantic magazine cover story about the woman who raised him and his siblings under conditions that amounted to indentured servitude, “My Family’s Slave.” Tizon’s pieces brim with enlightening details about people who existed outside the mainstream’s field of vision.

“Tizon’s talent was in seeking out those with stories to tell outside of the mainstream: those who don’t quite fit in, whether because of their race, religion, ethnicity, or lifestyle. Easy answers don’t exist in Invisible People; each piece leaves the audience a little bit unsettled, wanting more. The collection may focus on those who are invisible, but Tizon’s writing was anything but.”

— Foreword Reviews

The Great Migration and the Democratic Party: Black Voters and the Realignment of American Politics in the 20th Century

Keneshia N. Grant

Where Black people live has long been an important determinant of their ability to participate in political processes. In addition to its social and economic impacts, the Great Migration significantly changed the way Democratic Party elites interacted with Black communities in the northern cities of Detroit, New York, and Chicago. Many white Democratic politicians came to believe the growing pool of Black voters could help them reach their electoral goals and these Black migrants built coalitions with local parties as they became politically active. By focusing on the Great Migration’s significant political consequences for U.S. cities, Grant goes where few scholars have gone before and lays the groundwork for ways of thinking about the contemporary impact of Black migration on American politics.

Monument Lab: Creative Speculations for Philadelphia

Edited by Paul M. Farber and Ken Lum

What is an appropriate monument for the current city of Philadelphia? That was the question posed by the curators, artists, scholars, and students who comprise the Philadelphia-based public art and history studio Monument Lab. And in 2017, along with Mural Arts Philadelphia, they produced and organized a groundbreaking, city-wide exhibition of temporary, site-specific works that engaged directly with the community. The installations, by a cohort of diverse artists considering issues of identity, appeared in iconic public squares and neighborhood parks with research and learning labs and prototype monuments. Monument Lab is both a catalog of the exhibition and a critical reflection on the proceedings, including contributions from interlocutors and collaborators. A stunning achievement, the exhibition prompted civic dialogue about public art and history around what it means to be a Philadelphian, a conversation that remains especially relevant as we consider how to reflect on the meaning of social justice.

Good Reasons to Run: Women and Political Candidacy

Edited by Shauna L. Shames, Rachel I. Bernhard, Mirya R. Holman, and Dawn Langan Teele

After the 2016 U.S. Presidential election, a large cohort of women emerged to run for office. Their efforts changed the landscape of candidates and representation. However, women are still far less likely than men to seek elective office, and face biases and obstacles in campaigns. The editors and contributors to Good Reasons to Run, a mix of scholars and practitioners who are leaders in the field of women and politics, examine the reasons why women run—and do not run—for political office. They focus on the opportunities, policies, and structures that promote women’s candidacies and ask not just who wants to run, but how to activate and encourage such ambition among a larger, diverse population of potential female candidates.

Public Programs

Beyond the Page, the Libraries’ free public programming series, fosters conversations of social, scholarly, and educational value as we invite artists, writers, scholars, and experts in a variety of fields to address topics of importance to Temple and the surrounding community.

This past academic year, the opening of the new Charles Library inspired us to explore the history and future of the book. We examined how books have evolved, how libraries adapt to meet user needs, and what Charles Library makes possible for our staff and patrons.

In addition to this curated series, we offered a variety of panels, collaborations, workshops, performances, and lectures, such as our award-winning Beyond the Notes concerts and long-running Chat in the Stacks programs.

And while we had to postpone some of our spring programs amid the closing of the library buildings, we were able to swiftly move a number of events and workshops online in order to continue to offer academic and cultural programs to our users.

Faculty and graduate students delve into the history and future of the book

We invited several faculty members, graduate students, and alumni to Charles Library to discuss their own research on that most iconic of library resources: the book. We held two events—one in the fall and one in the spring—and heard about work being done at the Tyler School of Art and Architecture, the Lewis Katz School of Medicine, and in the English department via a series of fascinating lightning talks. SCRC staff also prepared pop-up exhibits featuring archival materials as part of these talks.

Building the 21st century library: reflections on a project completed

In 2016, we launched a multi-year program series in collaboration with the Tyler School of Art and Architecture to explore the process of building a 21st century library as we embarked on our own project here at Temple. And this past spring, we held the culminating event in this series in our new Charles Library event space with the architects, project managers, and other professionals involved in the design and construction of the new building reflecting on their work and the finished project.

Hands-on workshops exploring data art

As part of Love Data Week in February, the Ginsburg Health Sciences Library invited artists Mina Zarfsaz and Angela McQuillan to lead a hands-on workshop exploring the idea that we need to interpret data for it to have a cultural value. Participants learned how data can be represented using sound and created their own art. Later that week Mina Zarfsaz also gave a talk and led a demo with her students at Charles Library on Main Campus.

A virtual Juneteenth celebration: in memory of Bootsie Barnes

The Charles L. Blockson Afro-American Collection moved its annual Juneteenth program to Zoom this year for a virtual celebration of Black Music Month featuring the late Philadelphia jazz legend Robert “Bootsie” Barnes. The program featured Bootsie’s wife Sandra Turner Barnes, a performance by Bootsie’s grandson Reginald Lewis, and video of Bootsie’s performance at the Blockson Collection in 2014.

Pop-up concert in Charles

Charles Library has opened up new possibilities in programming for us, one of which is the ability to hold events in different areas throughout the building. In the fall, musicians from the Philadelphia Orchestra set up in the atrium and performed a free pop up chamber concert for visitors to the library.

A student needed help getting started on finding published research in the Speech, Language, and Hearing Sciences. The librarian staffing Temple Libraries’ chat service, Sarah Jones, directed them to the libraries’ Communication Sciences and Disorders guide, which was exactly what they needed to jumpstart their research. The student wrote that the chat experience “couldn’t have been better. [The librarian] quickly and efficiently directed me to my intended resource with no delay. Much appreciated.”

While campus was closed, School of Social Work students working on literature reviews met with the social work librarian, Olivia Given Castello, via Zoom for advice on their research strategies for finding intervention studies on trauma treatment effectiveness, and tips on finding the full text of articles. One student wrote, “I only wish I had gone to her BEFORE I started my paper.”

To increase support of the Temple Health Sciences Center patrons, Health Sciences Libraries staff moved workshops online at the start of the pandemic. They also scheduled new workshops in increasing numbers. Attendees enjoyed the opportunity to attend remotely and found the sessions engaging and well produced. Comments included:

“Great online transition—really I had no idea it was the first online presentation of this workshop since it ran so smoothly!”

“Zoom was an effective method to present this information. It went very smoothly and the polls were effective. I think that this is a good method for presentation in the future and would allow for larger groups to sit in on the information.”

The patron later wrote, “for me, as [a] recent alum, the library resources have been valuable as a resource for entrepreneurship and career development.” Their follow-up feedback was also glowing, “You can’t improve on perfection!”
